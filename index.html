Task One

Build the Squad.

Wizz is an anti-social-media social app. No followers, no likes, no feed — just 1:1 conversations with strangers who get you. That brand promise shapes which creators fit, and which don't.

Strategic Framing · Three guiding principles
01

Content must mirror the product.

FaceTime with your funniest friend. Not a polished lifestyle feed.

02

Bias authenticity over reach.

Wizz's growth data shows micros drive installs; megas drive awareness. Budget accordingly.

03

Diversify from existing Wizz creators.

To crack 18+ female Gen Z, we need a distinct voice set: female-led, friendship-coded, aged up.

Task Two

Content Strategy.

Three competitors dominate different content registers. Let's analyze their different approaches.

Part 01 · Competitor Analysis
Direct Competitor

Yubo.

  • Paris-based social discovery app
  • 80M+ lifetime signups
  • Live video, swipe-based matching
  • Pivoted to 18+ in 2024 (92% user vote)
  • Target: 18–25 international
What content works for them, and why

Yubo's paid content is quiet on US TikTok. Cultural footprint is mostly organic — user storytimes about going viral on livestreams, memes about "Yubo rooms at 2am," occasional moral panic. Their paid playbook leans on in-product social proof: clips of live rooms with young users. Implicit promise: "this is what's happening right now on Yubo." Influencer work skews mid-tier lifestyle and music. Safety-forward PR (AI moderation, age ID) dominates owned channels — correct for teen heritage, but reads serious and product-focused, not fun.

Yubo's content is product demonstration. Wizz's should be emotional authenticity. They just tell you what the app does; Wizz tells you how it feels when you're on it.

Adjacent Competitor

Bumble
BFF.

  • Friendship mode of Bumble · 2016
  • "Making friends as an adult"
  • ~40% of user base is Gen Z
  • Target: 22–35, urban, female
  • Strong in life-transition moments
What works for them, and why

Bumble BFF owns one content pillar on TikTok: "making friends in your 20s is so hard" confessional storytime. Three-beat format: name the pain → joke about it → show the solution working. The hashtag ecosystem (#makingfriendsasadults, #friendshipapps, #bumblebff) drives install intent consistently. Their influencer strategy leans into IRL activations — brunches, spin classes, community events that become content. Sharp: it gives creators a moment to post, not a feature to describe.

Wizz's chat-first architecture (not swipe-first) is a real product differentiator. Every creator who says "I didn't have to swipe, I just started talking" pulls install intent out of Bumble BFF.

Direct Competitor

Feels.

  • French-origin hybrid app
  • Recently entered US market
  • "Strictly under 30" age cap
  • "More than a dating app"
  • Story-first profiles, no binary swiping
Three patterns in their content

Feels is Wizz's closest philosophical twin — same French origin, same anti-swipe ethos, same "not just a dating app" framing.

Generational-belonging copy is their strongest lever. The "628 weeks between 18 and 30" tagline turns a finite window into urgency. "If you're over 30, this isn't for you" — which paradoxically makes under-30s feel claimed.

Story-based profiles are the hook. Profiles are TikTok-grid-shaped. Creator partnerships become "here's what my Feels profile looks like." The profile is the creative.

Feels' "more than a dating app" framing is also its weakness — every user has to mentally pre-declare why they're there. For a 22-year-old who isn't sure what direction they want to take with this new, exciting conversation (platonic or more-than-friends), that ambiguity is friction. Wizz gives the freedom of choice; no pressure to make any decisions, just fun, free-flowing conversation.

Three Key Learnings
01

Engagement rate beats follower count as an install predictor.

For a friendship app, ER is the KPI we should filter on upstream — before even talking to creators about rates.

02

Megas are a reach tool, not a conversion tool.

Megas earn their rate when the KPI is awareness, cultural credibility, or celebrity association — not CPI-driven installs. This campaign measured installs. Megas shouldn't have taken 80% of budget.

03

View rate is the most underrated upstream metric.

View-to-follower ratio signals whether the algorithm pushed the content onto FYP. @vibesonly's 22.5% means 77.5% of her audience never saw it. Going forward: set a 35% view-rate floor for any paid creator, regardless of tier.

Task Three · Part Two

Campaign Review.

Two ads. Two different strategic reads. One sounds like a friend texting you. The other sounds like a brand pretending to be a friend.

"POV: me checking my Wizz requests"

Video 01 · 15s · UGC-style

Scroll-stopping content with a bold on-screen caption, trending sound, and an organic-looking feel.

What works
  • The trending sound resonates with Gen Z
  • On-screen caption is simple, readable, non-commercial
  • Quick and direct — matches Gen Z attention span
  • Concept telegraphs the product: guy on phone laughing, you understand Wizz is a messaging app in 3 seconds
  • The influencer's look (oversized logo hoodie, sagging pants) wouldn't fly in most brand campaigns — which is exactly what conceals that this is one
  • Bedroom + LED lights setting reads as real, not staged or overly aesthetic
What doesn't work
  • Insulting someone's appearance is increasingly an ick for Gen Z — reads as insecure and unnecessary
  • Platform risk: TikTok's trust & safety filters now aggressively throttle content that pattern-matches to harassment
  • Negative-connotation words as the main caption immediately frame Wizz itself as negative — viewers feel they'd be judged if they joined
Specific Improvement
Keep the energy, change the target. The "ew" sound is salvageable — point it at a competitor instead: "Ew, you're still using [competitor app]?" Same scroll-stop, none of the brand-safety risk, and Gen Z loves some brand beef.

"Download Wizz at your own risk"

Video 02 · 20s · Reverse psychology

Reverse psychology is proven with Gen Z — but it has to land natural, not engineered.

What works
  • "Nobody knows about this yet" turns Wizz into a gatekept hidden gem — in a culture where gatekept knowledge is the highest compliment
  • The "addiction" claim works similarly. "This app is ruining my sleep" doesn't register as a warning — it's the highest endorsement a product can get. The app is so compelling it breaks your routine, packaged as a problem
What doesn't work
  • The delivery feels scripted. Over-exaggerated shock immediately signals "not believable"
  • Opening with "If you see this app…" + the logo in the first 3 seconds = red flag that screams ad. Gen Z is fluent in brand briefing conventions, and this checks every box
  • Caption style — bold white ALL CAPS with heavy black stroke — screams promotion. Organic videos use lowercase plain white text with minimal styling
Specific Improvement
Lean harder into the consequences. The script tells you the app is addictive — the stronger version shows it. Creator looks slightly wrecked: undone hair, no makeup, the 2am look. A line like "my screen time is shocking but I honestly can't stop, this app has me in a chokehold" lands because it's specific and visual. Physical evidence makes it believable.
